10 greatest black opera singers of all time

July 18, 2024
blog

Opera singers are some of the most celebrated artists in the entertainment industry. Most of them have travelled widely and performed in famous halls. Some may be dead, but their contribution to music is still valued to date. So, who are some of the greatest black opera singers of all time?

Making it to the top is not an easy task. It requires determination, focus and hard work. That is why those who make it to the top are celebrated all over.

Top 10 greatest black opera singers

It is not easy to prepare a list of the best black opera singers of all time, considering that the world has had plenty of them in the history of opera. Additionally, different opera lovers have their own preference lists.

Nonetheless, here are the names of some of the best singers under this category, who have provided fans with unforgettable moments.

1. Paul Robeson

Paul Leroy Robeson was an accomplished performer of the 20th century. He is famous for the production of The Emperor Jones and Othello.

With his unmistakable bass-baritone voice, Paul first became an international sensation in the London premiere of Show Boat in 1928. He then produced Ol’ Man River, which became his signature song.

Steve McQueen, an Oscar-winning director, once described him as “the renaissance man of the 20th century". He used some songs to speak out against racism and became a world activist. He died in 1978, at the age of 78 years.

2. Camilla Williams

Camilla Ella Williams was one of the most famous black female opera singers in the United States. In 1946, she became the first black woman to appear in a leading role with a major American opera company — in the title role of “Madame Butterfly”.

She also sang the national anthem at the 1963 March on Washington. Throughout her music career, Camilla performed in various states of the United States, as well as in Asia and Australia.

Upon her retirement, she was the first African-American to be appointed as Professor of Voice at Indiana University. She died in 2012, at the age of 92 years.

3. Pretty Yende

Born in the year 1985 in a remote town in South Africa, Pretty was first introduced to operatic music at the age of sixteen. This happened when she listened to the Flower Duet from Delibes's opera Lakme in a British Airways commercial.

Her passion for operatic music grew, and she decided to become an opera singer. She obtained her Performer’s Diploma in Opera and a Post Graduate studies in Opera (Performance) at the South African College of Music (University of Cape Town), South Africa.

Her operatic repertoire includes Helena in Benjamin Britten’s A midsummer night’s dream, Fiordiligi in Mozart’s Cosi Fan Tutte, La Contessa in Mozart’s Le Nozze Di Figaro, Magda in Puccini’s La Rondine.

4. J’Nai Bridges

The American mezzo-soprano, J'Nai Bridges is one of the first rising and great black female opera singers. She is from Lakewood, Washington, and she studied at the Manhattan School of Music and the Curtis Institute of Music.

Her passion for music was evident from childhood. While in school, she performed Idamante Idomeneo, title role Carmen in Peter Brook’s La tragédie de Carmen and Baba the Turk The Rake’s Progress.

She has so far achieved a lot in her music career, including the 2012 Marian Anderson award, prizes at the 2012 and 2011 Metropolitan Opera National Council Auditions, the 2013 Sullivan Award, 2013 Union League Award, and 2011 Sara Tucker Study Grant.

5. George Shirley

Shirley was born on April 18th, 1934 in Indianapolis, Indiana, to Irving and Daisy Shirley. He began performing by joining his mother and father to form a musical trio within the Indianapolis church community.

When his parents moved to Detroit, Michigan, Shirley continued to build his musical talents. He played the baritone horn in a community band and studied voice while he was a student at Northern High School.

Fortunately, his musical talent earned him a scholarship to Wayne State University. He graduated from the university with a B.S. in Music Education. In 1961, George was the first Black singer to win the Metropolitan Opera National Council Auditions.

6. Kathleen Battle

Born on August 13th, 1948, in Portsmouth, Ohio, Kathleen is considered one of the greatest black female opera singers of her time. She studied music education at the University of Cincinnati College-Conservatory of Music.

Much as she was encouraged to seek a vocal career, Battle took a teaching position at a Cincinnati inner-city public school. She taught music to kids aged between 10 to 12 years and occasionally competed in music audition.

Kathleen's early performances won her critical and public acclaim and led her to work with her mentor, James Levine.

7. Denyce Graves

Denyce is recognized worldwide as one of today's most exciting vocal celebrities. She continues to gather unparalleled popularity and critical acclaim in performances in the United States and beyond.

In the United States, Graves is identified as "an operatic superstar of the 21st century". Some of her fans have even described her voice as that which has the power to move them.

Her career has taken her to the world's great opera houses and concert halls. The combination of her expressive, rich vocalism, elegant stage presence, and exciting theatrical understanding allows her to pursue a wide breadth of operatic portrayals.

8. Willard White

Sir Willard White is a Jamaican born bass-baritone. He made his professional operatic debut in 1974 with New York City Opera, where he played the character of Colline in La Bohème.

The singer was born on October 10th, 1946, in St. Catherine, Jamaica, the son of a dockworker, Egbert, and Gertrude White. His reputation as a singer rests on his warm, powerful voice and his stage presence.

His career is equally remarkable because of the range of music he has performed successfully. He is recognized internationally and has won numerous awards.

9. William Warfield

William Caesar Warfield was born in West Helena, Arkansas, on January 22nd, 1920. The great American bass-baritone was famous for his great talent and his marriage to the soprano Leontyne Price.

William was one of the most famous African American opera singers of his time. Unfortunately, he died in 2002, at the age of 82 years.

10. Jessye Norman

American operatic soprano, Jessy Norman was one of the finest of her day. Norman was reared in a musical family. Both her mother and grandmother were pianists, and her father sang in church, as did the young Jessye.

Jessye won a scholarship to Howard University in Washington, D.C., where she studied voice. She graduated in 1967 and received further training at the Peabody Conservatory in Baltimore, Maryland, and at the University of Michigan.

Jessye Norman enjoyed success as a recitalist with her thorough scholarship and her ability to project drama through her voice. She enjoyed a successful concert career.
